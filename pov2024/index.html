A Short Overview of Suicide Rates in Western North Carolina and the Wider Queer Community, and the Positive Outcomes Associated With Outreach and Community-Building

This presentation is designed to outline the structures that contribute to high rates of suicide in counties along the Tennessee border of Western North Carolina, as well as facilitate a larger discussion examining high rates of suicide among those in the LGBTQ+ community. In doing so, this presentation does not attempt to draw any concrete connections in the form of causation or correlation between the two topics. Rather, a shared space is given to both in order to propose further research to fill in the gaps that exist within the two spheres.

Background

Studies that utilize surveys to assess suicide risk in the US have revealed that LGBTQ+ folk experience a greater risk compared to those that identify as heterosexual, straight, and/or cis-gendered.1, 2

In the CDC’s Youth Risk Behavior Survey (YRBS), students are asked several questions in regard to their experiences within the last 12 months. Among these are questions related to feelings of sadness/hopelessness, seriously considering suicide, making a suicide plan, attempting suicide, and/or making a suicide attempt that required treatment. Results from these surveys have shown that while 5% of heterosexual/straight/cis-gendered students have attempted suicide, 23% of LGBTQ+ students have.1

The Southern LGBTQ Experiences Survey conducted by Campaign for Southern Equality and Campus Pride similarly asked questions related to mental health and suicide risk. Among the questions asked are those related to experiencing suicidal ideation or serious suicidal thoughts, attempting suicide, engaging in self-harming behaviors, engaging in disordered eating, self-isolating from support networks, and being hospitalized/staying in a mental health facility for safety reasons. Across the survey’s participants, 56.21% had experienced suicidal ideation, and 13.52% had attempted suicide.2

As of December 2023, an estimated 4.4% of North Carolinians identify as being a part of the LGBTQ+ community, which equates to roughly 353,100 people state-wide. 3

Data shows that many counties with the highest rates of suicide are found in the Appalachian region of Western North Carolina on the Tennessee border, including Cherokee, Swain, Mitchell, Ashe, and Avery. Nestled in the hollers of the mountains, isolated in small, rural towns, exist many structural barriers to health care. The following is a list of structures that contribute to Western North Carolina’s high suicide rates.

Barriers to Access Mental Healthcare in Western NC

Geographical isolation: The Appalachian landscape lends itself to the difficulties of accessing healthcare services. Distance to counseling/treatment centers7,8, shortage of providers in the area9, and limited access to transporation10 are among the most impactful. Additionally, limited availability to broadband internet can affect many people’s accesses to virtual resources as well.1

Isolation, Economics, and a Harsh Natural Environment

Social isolation: Appalachia has a long history of individualism and self-reliance4, as well as skepticism towards “outsiders,” whose definition extends to mental health care providers5,6. Strong religious/spiritual ties also contribute to cultural health practices of folk medicine, many of which are not supplemented with biomedical interventions.13
Economic challenges: Using factors such as unemployment rates, household incomes, and population growth, the NC Department of Commerce has designated Cherokee and Mitchell as Tier 1 most-distressed counties, and Swain, Avery, and Ashe as Tier 212.
